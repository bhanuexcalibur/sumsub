Product Understanding

Two products. Two audiences. One platform bet.

Reusable Identity isn't one product, it's a platform strategy. Sumsub is simultaneously building network effects on two sides: one B2B motion to get existing clients to adopt Reusable KYC, and one B2C motion to grow direct Sumsub ID sign-ups. Both require different messaging, different activation channels, and different success metrics.

B2B
Reusable KYC

Sold to existing Sumsub clients. The pitch: you share verified user data with other ecosystem partners upon user consent, skipping redundant checks and cutting your onboarding drop-off. A network-effects product that gets more valuable the more clients adopt it.

  • Primary buyer: compliance and product teams at fintechs, crypto exchanges
  • GTM motion: expansion revenue from existing client base, ecosystem partnerships
  • Key metric: Reusable KYC uptake rate among existing accounts
  • Biggest objection: data-sharing anxiety, GDPR liability questions
B2C
Sumsub ID

Signed up directly by end users. The pitch: verify once across 4,000+ platforms, never upload your passport again. A consumer-facing identity wallet that creates stickiness and reverse-funnel demand back to Sumsub's B2B clients.

  • Primary buyer: individual users in crypto, fintech, gig economy
  • GTM motion: direct acquisition, partnership distribution through Sumsub clients
  • Key metric: monthly active Sumsub ID users, repeat-use rate
  • Biggest objection: trust and data security skepticism from end consumers
Positioning Audit

What the positioning gets right, and where it leaves value on the table.

Sumsub's homepage and product pages carry a clear compliance-focused narrative that works well with regulated-industry buyers. The Reusable Identity launch adds new complexity: it requires two distinct stories running in parallel, targeting different people with different motivations. The current messaging blends them in ways that weaken both.

Observation The "full-cycle" frame is underused Sumsub's core claim is "full-cycle verification" but this rarely shows up as a strategic argument. It should be the main reason to stay with Sumsub across the customer journey, not just a product category label. Competitors like Persona and Veriff are narrower, and that's actually an opening.
Tension Three audiences, one homepage The current homepage simultaneously addresses developers evaluating the API, compliance teams buying KYC/AML, and now consumer end-users signing up for Sumsub ID. These three audiences have completely different intent signals and don't share a common page goal. Someone Googling "reusable KYC" and landing on the Sumsub homepage has to work to find what they came for.
Opportunity Ecosystem scale is the real moat "4,000+ platforms" is mentioned in press releases but buried on the product page. This is the Reusable Identity network effect in plain numbers. For a user comparing Sumsub ID to a competitor identity wallet, that number means your verified identity actually works everywhere. Lead with it.
Observation Clients like Duolingo and Vodafone signal something Having Duolingo and Vodafone alongside Bitpanda and Bybit in the same client logo row is actually a positioning statement: Sumsub is cross-industry. Most KYC vendors are perceived as "fintech tools." The diversity of the client base should be working harder in the narrative.
Gap The launch narrative led with features, not the behavior change The Reusable Identity press release framed the story as "onboarding times by 50%, conversion rates by 30%." These are platform owner metrics. The more compelling consumer story, "your personal data is verified once and travels with you," barely appeared. Two audiences needed two different angles, not one headline.
Gap GDPR and consent got one sentence For any data-sharing product in Europe, consent and compliance are the first objections, not afterthoughts. The launch copy mentions GDPR compliance almost in passing. A dedicated trust section, not buried in a FAQ, would have removed the main hesitation from every compliance buyer reading the announcement.
Opportunity The "1 in 3" stat is powerful and underplayed Sumsub's own data shows one in three applicants has already verified through Sumsub before. That number is the most visceral proof that Reusable KYC is a real problem worth solving, not a product innovation in search of a use case. It should be the opening line of every piece of Reusable Identity content.
Observation Distribution gaps: where did the launch actually land? The press release reached general tech media. For Reusable KYC's actual buyers, fintech compliance teams and product leads at exchanges, the relevant trade publications (Finextra, The Block, Payments Dive) and community channels (compliance Slack groups, fintech newsletters) needed dedicated seeding. That layer isn't visible from the public launch.
Observation Onfido (now Entrust IDV): enterprise trust, limited ecosystem Onfido's acquisition by Entrust gives it serious enterprise credibility in banking and government. The trade-off: Entrust is a legacy player, which typically means slower product velocity and a more conservative roadmap. Against Sumsub's full-cycle approach, Onfido can't easily offer a reusable identity layer across a 4,000-platform ecosystem.
Observation Veriff: global document coverage, limited network Veriff's strength is breadth of document coverage across markets. Their GTM leans toward global consumer apps and marketplaces. The Reusable Identity angle is not a Veriff play right now. Sumsub's competitive frame should acknowledge Veriff's global reach but position the ecosystem network as an asset Veriff simply doesn't have.
Observation Persona: flexibility without scale Persona competes on workflow customization, the "build your own KYC flow" pitch. That's a strong argument for product teams who want control. But Persona doesn't have a reusable identity story. A Sumsub client considering Persona needs to understand that customization and portability are not the same product value.
Opportunity Sumsub's frame: "compliance infrastructure, not just verification" None of the core competitors are leading with an ecosystem or network play. Sumsub is the only platform positioned to offer both the compliance tooling and the reusable user credential. That's a genuinely differentiated wedge. The GTM story should be: other vendors check identity. Sumsub makes verified identity portable.
Gap The network effect is not being explained as a strategic story Reusable KYC becomes more valuable as more clients join the ecosystem: more users have existing Sumsub credentials, so more onboardings can skip full verification. But this flywheel is not surfaced in the current messaging. Clients considering adoption don't see why timing matters, or why being an early adopter in the ecosystem is an advantage.
Opportunity Build the "ecosystem growth" narrative for sales Every new Reusable KYC client adds their user base to the shared verification pool, which benefits all existing clients too. This is a classic platform network effect. It should have its own one-pager, its own data visualization in the sales deck, and ideally a public dashboard showing ecosystem growth. Mercuryo's published case study (reduced onboarding time across 200+ ecosystem companies) is the template.
Tension Consumer trust is the prerequisite Sumsub ID hasn't fully solved publicly Users sharing their identity credential across 4,000 platforms is only compelling if they trust the platform holding that credential. Sumsub's B2B reputation is strong, but B2C trust is built differently: through visible user testimonials, clear data control messaging, and a consumer-facing privacy story that currently doesn't exist at the same quality as the compliance messaging.
Opportunity Activate the existing user base as a distribution channel Every time a Sumsub client onboards a user, that user is a potential Sumsub ID sign-up. Currently that touchpoint is only used for verification. Adding a "save your identity to Sumsub ID" prompt post-verification is an acquisition motion that costs nothing to run and scales with every client. The users are already there.
GTM Brief

How I'd run the Reusable Identity GTM motion in 90 days.

The B2B and B2C motions are at different stages of maturity and need different resources. The B2B Reusable KYC play is an expansion revenue problem: you have 4,000 clients, most of whom aren't on Reusable KYC yet. The B2C Sumsub ID play is an acquisition problem: you need consumer trust and organic distribution. Here's how I'd sequence the work.

Month 1 · Foundation
Fix the positioning layer first
Separate the B2B and B2C narratives: Reusable KYC gets a dedicated landing page, Sumsub ID gets its own page with consumer-facing copy
Write a one-pager for sales: "The case for Reusable KYC adoption" with the ecosystem network effect clearly explained
Surface the "1 in 3 users already verified" stat across all Reusable Identity content: homepage, sales materials, social
Audit the existing Reusable KYC client list and identify the 10 best candidates for a new case study
Month 2 · Activation
Drive Reusable KYC uptake from existing accounts
Launch an in-product prompt: clients not on Reusable KYC see a "your users are already verified elsewhere" banner in the dashboard
Run a Customer Success-driven campaign: CS teams get a short talk track and a co-sell incentive to surface Reusable KYC in QBRs
Publish the ecosystem growth stat: number of users with reusable credentials in the ecosystem, updated monthly. Make it a public trust signal.
Trade press seeding: Finextra, Payments Dive, The Block. Pitch the "verification fatigue" story as a sector problem with a named data point.
Month 3 · Scale
Build the Sumsub ID consumer acquisition engine
Add a post-verification Sumsub ID sign-up prompt across all client touchpoints: "Verified? Save your identity for next time."
Partner distribution: identify 5 high-volume Sumsub clients willing to co-promote Sumsub ID to their user base in exchange for faster onboarding data
Consumer trust content: publish "How Sumsub ID protects your data" as a dedicated page. Answer GDPR questions in plain language.
Review first 90 days: activation metrics, Reusable KYC uptake rate, Sumsub ID sign-up volume. Adjust channel mix based on what's converting.
Key assumption to validate early

The B2B and B2C motions can reinforce each other, but only if the consumer trust layer (Sumsub ID data control, privacy UX) is strong enough. If early Sumsub ID users have negative experiences around data transparency, that creates noise that makes the Reusable KYC B2B sell harder. Fix the consumer trust story before scaling acquisition.

Growth Experiments

Rapid experiments I'd run across the Reusable Identity funnel.

These aren't theoretical campaign ideas. They're specific, testable hypotheses with clear success metrics. The goal is to find what actually drives Reusable KYC adoption and Sumsub ID sign-ups before committing to scaled channels.

Acquisition
Post-verification Sumsub ID prompt

A user who just completed KYC on any Sumsub client platform is at peak awareness of identity friction. Adding a "save for next time" prompt at that moment should convert significantly better than cold acquisition.

Test: prompt vs no prompt Metric: Sumsub ID sign-up rate Duration: 30 days
Acquisition
Ecosystem size as a conversion lever

Does showing the number of Sumsub ecosystem platforms on the Sumsub ID landing page increase sign-up intent? Hypothesis: "works across 4,000+ platforms" is more compelling than generic "verify once, use everywhere" copy.

Test: A/B copy variants Metric: landing page CVR Duration: 3 weeks
Activation
Reusable KYC dashboard nudge for existing clients

Clients who have never enabled Reusable KYC likely haven't because of activation friction, not lack of interest. A contextual banner in the Sumsub dashboard ("X of your users have already verified with Sumsub elsewhere") should increase exploration rate.

Test: banner vs no banner Metric: Reusable KYC settings page visits Duration: 45 days
Activation
CS talk track: "Your drop-off is a Reusable KYC problem"

Clients experiencing high onboarding drop-off rates are a natural Reusable KYC audience. Equipping CS with a data-backed talk track that attributes drop-off to re-verification friction (using Sumsub's own aggregate data) should increase Reusable KYC adoption conversations.

Test: CS pilot cohort vs control Metric: Reusable KYC demo requests Duration: 60 days
Retention
Sumsub ID "usage activity" email sequence

Users who sign up for Sumsub ID but don't experience a reuse event within 30 days are likely to churn. An email sequence triggered by "no reuse event" that shows them how many platforms they could use their ID on should reactivate passive users.

Test: triggered sequence vs no email Metric: D30 repeat usage rate Duration: 45 days
Retention
Trust transparency page for Sumsub ID

Consumer data storage hesitation is the main reason identity wallets see high sign-up but low retention. A dedicated "your data, your control" page with explicit GDPR rights, deletion flow, and sharing audit trail should reduce churn from privacy-conscious users.

Test: page exists vs no page Metric: D60 active user retention Duration: 60 days
Copy Sample

How I'd write the Reusable KYC B2B landing page hero.

The current Reusable KYC page leads with feature description. Here's a version that leads with the buyer's actual problem and lets the product answer it.

Reusable KYC · Landing Page Hero · Revised
One in three of your applicants has already passed KYC. Somewhere.

Right now, those users are uploading their passport again, going through liveness checks again, and waiting for approval again. Not because you need to verify them. Because your systems don't know they've already been verified.

Reusable KYC fixes this. When a user has completed KYC with any other platform in the Sumsub ecosystem, your onboarding inherits that verified status, with their consent, and within your compliance framework. Full compliance. Instant onboarding.

Why this works: The original copy leads with "eliminate repetitive verification." This version leads with the buyer's current state, forcing them to picture the problem in their own onboarding flow before the product is even named. "One in three" is Sumsub's own verified data point and it's specific enough to create credibility while being universally recognizable to anyone who runs a fintech platform. The phrase "your systems don't know" puts the blame on the process, not the user, which is the framing compliance and product teams actually respond to.
